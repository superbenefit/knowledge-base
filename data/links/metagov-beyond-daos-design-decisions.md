This Metagov framework challenges the assumption that all internet organizations should adopt DAO structures, offering comprehensive analysis of governance design choices across coordination models. Rather than treating DAOs as universal solution, the work examines when token-weighted governance, representative democracy, liquid democracy, futarchy, or other mechanisms serve specific organizational needs and values. Metagov identifies key design dimensions—[decision-making](data/concepts/decisions.md) authority, membership boundaries, proposal processes, execution mechanisms—showing how different combinations create distinct governance systems with different tradeoffs. The framework enables organizations to make intentional design choices aligned with their goals rather than defaulting to popular patterns that may not fit their contexts.

### Key Highlights

- **Beyond DAO Defaults**: Metagov demonstrates that "DAO" encompasses vast design space with fundamentally different governance models, challenging organizations to specify what structures actually serve their needs rather than adopting token-weighted governance because it's associated with web3 legitimacy.

- **Governance Design Dimensions**: The framework identifies key choice points in governance systems—who can participate, how decisions are proposed, what voting mechanisms determine outcomes, how execution is enforced—showing how these dimensions combine to create coordination systems with distinct properties and tradeoffs.

- **Context-Dependent Structures**: Rather than advocating universal best practices, Metagov shows how organizational purpose, community size, decision types, and values should guide governance design—what works for protocol parameter adjustment differs from community resource allocation or strategic direction-setting.

- **Mechanism Tradeoffs**: The work examines advantages and limitations of different governance mechanisms—token voting's capital efficiency versus plutocratic capture, one-person-one-vote's democratic legitimacy versus sybil vulnerability, delegated authority's operational effectiveness versus capture risk—enabling informed tradeoff assessment.

- **Interoperability and Composability**: Metagov explores how governance mechanisms can interoperate, allowing organizations to use different structures for different decisions rather than forcing all choices through single mechanism—treasury management might use different process than community norms or technical parameters.

- **Empirical Grounding**: The framework draws on Metagov's research across functioning internet organizations, providing evidence about what approaches work in practice rather than theoretical speculation about ideal governance.

### Practical Applications

This framework enables intentional governance design across contexts:

- Internet organizations can use Metagov's dimensions to audit current governance structures, identifying whether mechanisms align with organizational values and needs or merely replicate popular patterns without intentional design

- DAO developers can apply the framework when designing governance systems, making explicit choices about participation boundaries, decision-making authority, and execution mechanisms rather than defaulting to token-weighted voting

- Platform cooperatives can reference the analysis when creating member governance, understanding how different mechanisms serve cooperative principles better than structures borrowed from corporate or blockchain contexts

- Community organizers can draw on empirical evidence about governance approaches when proposing structures, showing what has worked in similar contexts rather than advocating based on theoretical ideals

- Researchers can build on Metagov's design dimensions when studying internet governance, creating shared vocabulary for comparing different coordination systems and identifying patterns across contexts
